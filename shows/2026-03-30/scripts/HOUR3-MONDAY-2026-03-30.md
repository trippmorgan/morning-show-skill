### SEGMENT 2: THIS DAY IN MUSIC — PRINCE & SIGN O' THE TIMES (7:13 AM)
**[WKRP TRANSITION: 3 sec]**

> This day in music. Nineteen eighty seven. Prince releases Sign of the Times. His ninth studio album. Let that number sit with you for a second. Ninth. By nineteen eighty seven. The man was twenty eight years old and had made nine albums.
>
> Now here is the thing about this record. Prince had just broken up with the Revolution. His band. The people who helped him make Purple Rain. And he wanted to release a triple album called Crystal Ball. Warner Brothers said no. They said two discs. Prince, being Prince, did not argue. He just edited it down to a double album and made it perfect anyway.
>
> The title track is one of the most important songs of the eighties. It is Prince looking at the world and just... listing what he sees. AIDS. Gangs. Crack. The space shuttle. Hurricane Annie. He is not preaching. He is not offering solutions. He is just watching. And that is what makes it devastating. Sometimes the bravest thing an artist can do is just describe what is actually happening.
>
> Also on this date, nineteen ninety four. Pink Floyd begins the Division Bell tour in Miami. Their last tour ever. Thirty one years ago. If you were there, I am jealous. If you were not born yet, I am suddenly very aware of my age.
>
> Here is some more Prince. Because one song is never enough Prince.

### SEGMENT 4: THE WHITE STRIPES LIE (7:42 AM)

> Okay. Music history time. And this one is genuinely fun.
>
> On this date in two thousand and one, the Detroit Free Press published a story that blew up the entire mythology of the White Stripes. Jack and Meg White, the band, had been telling everyone, the press, the fans, the record labels, everyone, that they were brother and sister. Siblings. That was the story. Two weird kids from Detroit playing garage blues.
>
> Except they were not siblings. They were married. And then divorced. Court records showed they got married in nineteen ninety six and divorced in two thousand. And they had been playing the sibling card the entire time.
>
> Now here is what I love about this. Rolling Stone reported them as siblings. The New York Times reported them as siblings. Nobody checked. Nobody pulled the marriage license. Nobody said hey, these two people with the same last name who look weirdly similar, maybe we should Google this. Which, to be fair, Google was barely a thing in two thousand one. But still.
>
> And the best part? When the story broke, they never really addressed it. They just kind of shrugged and kept making music. Which is the most rock and roll response possible. "Yes, we lied about our entire relationship. Anyway, here is Seven Nation Army."
>
> What a band.
